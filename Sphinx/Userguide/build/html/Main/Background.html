Background#

What is MPCD ?#

In many situtations in soft matter physics, the fluid medium in which mesoscale particles—such as polymers, colloids or cells—are suspended is crucial but not the principle interest of study. In such cases, coarse-grained algorithms that simulate one or more aspect of the surrounding fluid are needed. For example, if the most significant effect of the fluid is to induce thermal fluctuations then a Langevin or Brownian dynamics simulation might be most suitable [Slater2009]. On the other hand, if the embedded particles are large enough that thermal fluctuations are negligible but hydrodynamic interactions between them are significant then an immersed boundary solver [Mittel2005] or lattice-Boltzmann simulation [Aidun2010] might be more appropriate.

  • If both fluctuations and hydrodynamic interactions are crucial then MPCD may be an appropriate simulation technique.

Multi-Particle Collision Dynamics (MPCD) is a technique for simulating fluctuating hydrodynamics that reproduces the Navier-Stokes equation on sufficiently long length and time acales [Pooley2005]. It is well suited for situations for which viscosity dominates over inertia, and diffusion is of similar significance to advective flow (see Dimensionless Numbers). It is straightforward to include mobile particles, such as colloids, within the MPCD fluid, even if these have many internal degress of freedom, such as polymers. This makes MPCD an excellent option for simulating complex suspensions. Furthermore, because MPCD is a particle-based method, non-trivial boundaries can be included more easily than methods that require complicated meshes.

As in any computational approach, these benefits are counterbalanced by complications, including

  1. fluid properties (such as viscosity or other transport coefficients) are only controlled indirectly,

  2. multiple runs must be averaged to find statisticly meaningful results, and

  3. compressibility [Zhao2013].

Particle-based coarse-graining#

In MPCD, the continuous fluid medium is discretised into point-like, coarse-grained fluid particles. Equivalently one may imagine that many fluid atoms or molecules are grouped together and simulated as point-like particles. This coarse-graning eschews molecular-scale details of the molecules that make up the fluid. Respecting conservation of these quantities is what allows the hydrodynamic equations of motion to be obeyed on sufficiently long length and time scales [Pooley2005].

Thus, MPCD does not give correct dynamics on microscopic time or length scales. Streaming and collision events conserve the momentum and energy in each collision volume at all times and so MPCD reproduces an accurate representation of the hydrodynamics of the velocity field at large distances. The coarse-grained mesoscopic MPCD fluid provides both a thermal bath and propagates hydrodynamic interactions without having to either solve the entire Navier-Stokes equation [Yeomans2006] for a potentially complex microfluidic system or to simulate the full molecular interactions of all fluid molecules. Therefore, it can be computationally less expensive than numerical, continuum solvers or full Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations.

Two-step algorithm#

In essence, the MPCD algorithm consists of only two steps:

  1. The first step is the streaming step.

  2. The second is the collision step.

Streaming step.

Collision step.

Streaming step#

Each fluid particle (labelled \(i\)) of mass \(m_i\) moves ballistically, and its position \(\vec{x}_i\left(t\right)\) is updated in discrete time intervals \(\delta t\). In the streaming step the particles move according to

(1)#\[\vec{x}_i\left(t+\delta t\right) = \vec{x}_i\left(t\right) + \vec{v}_i\left(t\right) \delta t .\]

If the MPCD algorithm consisted only of streaming steps then the carrier fluid would be an ideal gas; however, the collision step introduces coarse-grained interactions between particles. Particle properties are continuous—not discretised (see Particle Properties).

Collision step#

The second step is the collision step in which momentum is transferred between fluid particles. To exchange momentum the simulation domain is partitioned into \(d\)-dimensional square cells of size \(a\). After streaming, all particles are binned into cells (labelled \(c\)) and all the particles in a given cell are said to collide locally exchanging particle properties via a stochastic collision operation.

The collision step is typically a simple, non-physical scheme constructed to be stochastic but also obey appropraite conservation laws (such as conservation of momentum, angular momentum and energy) within each cell. Multi-particle collisions within cells are represented by the collision operator \(\vec{\Xi}\) such that the velocity of each particle in a given cell evolves as

(2)#\[\vec{v}_i\left(t + \delta t\right) = \vec{v}^{c}\left(t\right) + \vec{\Xi} \left( \; \vec{v}_i\left(t\right), \vec{v}^{c}\left(t\right) \; \right) .\]

The number of fluid particles in the \(c^\text{th}\) cell at time \(t\) is \({N_c}\left(t\right)\). The number of particles in each cell may vary from one cell to another but the total number \(N_\text{tot}\) is conserved and the average number density is \(n=\left\langle N_c \right\rangle/a^d\) where \(d\) is the dimensionality of the simulation and \(\left\langle \cdot \right\rangle\) is the average over all cells. Each cell possesses a centre of mass velocity \(\vec{v}^{c} = \left\langle v_i \right\rangle_c = \left(\sum^{N_c}_{i=1} m_i \vec{v}_i\right)/\left(\sum^{N_c}_{i=1} m_i\right)\), where \(\left\langle \cdot \right\rangle_c\) represents the average within cell \(c\). The cell velocity \(\vec{v}^{c}\) corresponds to the local macroscopic velocity \(\vec{v}\left(\vec{x}_c,t\right)\) at the location of cell \(c\) and time \(t\). Thus, these cell-based values are interpretted as discretized field values (see Cell Properties).

The MPCD collision step replaces molecular-scale dynamics with a local, non-physical, stochastic momentum transfer operation. A number of collision operators are available (see Collision operators).

Units#

Measurements in this simulator are only relevant when compared to a few global values, described the fundamental units. In the derived units section, these units are combined to describe the natural units of the other main observables. In order to compare results with experiments or to identify operating regimes, measurements can be combined into dimensionless numbers: this process is described in the dimensionless numbers paragraph.

Fundamental units#

Mass (\(m\)): The mass of a fluid particle sets the natural unit of mass. A fluid particle here means a discretized packet of fluid material. In multiphase fluids, the mass of different species can be set to be different: this will change the unit of most observables between species.

Length (\(a\)): The cell size sets the natural unit of length.

Energy (\(k_B T\)): Temperature of the simulator’s thermostat, i.e. the thermal energy of the system.

Since these define every other unit, they are set to one for convenience.

Derived units#

Time: Derived from the equipartition theorem: \(a\sqrt{\frac{m}{k_B T}}\). Hereafter referred to as [τ]. An important quantity it defines is the timestep, δt, which is described in the introduction chapter.

Velocity: Length divided by time, so [v]= \(\sqrt{\frac{k_B T}{m}}\).

Acceleration: Velocity divided by time, so [a]= \(\frac{k_B T}{m a}\).

Force: Mass times acceleration, so [F]= \(\frac{k_B T}{a}\).

Diffusion coefficient: Length divided by time squared, so [D]= \(\frac{k_B T}{a m}\).

Rotational diffusion coefficient: Rotational diffusion coefficient divided by length squared, so [D R]= \(\frac{K_B T}{a^3 m}\).

Spring constant: Force divided by length, so [k] = \(\frac{k_B T}{a^2}\).

Number density: Number of particles divided by cell size, so [n]= \(a^{-d}\), where d is the number of dimensions in the system.

Mass density: Total mass of particles in a cell divided by cell size, so [ρ]= \(n m\), where d is the number of dimensions in the system.

Pressure: Force divided by length to the power of d-1, where d is the number of dimensions. [P]= \(\frac{k_B T}{a^d}\). Streaming and collisional pressures are added together.

Viscosity: Pressure multiplied by time, so [μ] = \(\frac{\sqrt{k_B T m}}{a^{d-1}}\). Changing the timestep will result in a change in effective viscosity. See this section for more details.

Coupling to the mean field potential: Energy divided by two space derivatives, so [U]= \(\frac{k_B T}{a^{d-2}}\). Used for nematic MPCD, see this section for more details.

Bending potential: Bending energy divided by cosine squared of the bending angle, so [k b]= \(k_B T\).

Activity: The unit of activity in this simulator is the same as the unit of force. Activity gives a contribution to the collision operator that is weighted by activity, the length of a timestep, and the mass of the particles. This results in units of force for the activity - see [Kozhukhov2022] for more details.

Dimensionless numbers#

Dimensionless numbers allow the user to find out the regime at which the simulator is operating. This allows for comparisons to theoretical regimes and to experiments.

Reynolds number#

The Reynolds number is an example of such a number. It broadly defines the ratio of inertia effects to viscous effects, and is defined as

\[Re=\frac{v L}{μ},\]

where L is a typical length scale for the system, v is a typical velocity, and μ is a typical viscosity.

In classical mechanics, turbulence is a phenomenon occuring at high Reynolds number, where excess energy is dissipated by chaotic energy cascades. Active nematics can give rise to low-Reynolds turbulence: this important result can be recreated with this simulator.

Péclet number#

The Péclet number defines the ratio of diffusive transport rate to advective transport rate, i.e. propulsive behaviour against thermal behaviour. It is defined as

\[Pe=\frac{v L}{D},\]

where L is a typical length scale, v is a typical velocity, and D is a typical diffusion coefficient.

Small values indicate a system dominated by diffusivity, and larger values indicate a system dominated by advection - MPCD typically operates at moderate Pe, where both behaviours are important.

Schmidt number#

The Schmidt number compares the relative importances of diffusion and viscosity. It is defined as

\[Sc=\frac{μ}{D ρ},\]

where μ is the viscosity, D is the diffusivity, and ρ is the density.

The Schmidt number offers a way to measure the compressibility of a fluid, broadly speaking how ‘gas-like’ this fluid is. In MPCD, hydrodynamic disturbances travel at a finite speed, while they are instantaneous in an classical fluid - this translates to a relatively low Sc. Particle-based methods (such as Molecular Dynamics, Lattice Boltzmann, Dissipative Particle Dynamics, or MPCD) simulate fluids that are not incompressible, which is a drawback [Slater2009]. However, this is not an issue if the applied forces or pressure gradients are limited to small values.

Mach number#

The Mach number relates the flow velocity past a boundary (v) to the local speed of sound (c). It is defined as

\[M=\frac{v}{c}.\]

In MPCD, the speed of sound is relatively low, which results in high Mach numbers. To be more precise, the speed of sound is \(\sqrt{\frac{2 k_B T}{m}}\), as defined in [Padding2004].

Warning

When using periodic boundary conditions, finite size effects come into play. As hydrodynamic interactions are long range, the simulated system can then interact with itself, potentially impacting measurements. This is important to keep in mind.
